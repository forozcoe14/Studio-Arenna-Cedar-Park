What is padel?

Padel is a racquet sport played on enclosed courts, typically in doubles. It combines elements of tennis and squash but in a more social, approachable format. The sport is reservation-based, community-oriented, and designed to keep play moving through structured time blocks — which makes padel clubs naturally predictable in their rhythm of arrivals and departures.

The court and its characteristics

A padel court is a rectangle enclosed by a combination of tempered glass and steel mesh. The playing surface is typically artificial turf with fine sand infill, which cushions movement and moderates ball bounce. Unlike tennis, the walls are part of play — the ball can legally rebound off them, which rewards angles, anticipation, and team coordination.

01

Doubles by design

Padel is primarily played 2 vs. 2. The enclosed court and shorter dimensions reward teamwork, communication, and tactical placement over raw power.

02

Walls are part of play

The ball can rebound off the glass walls. This changes the rhythm of the sport — extending rallies and creating a chess-like pattern of angles.

03

Scoring like tennis

Points follow the familiar tennis scoring system (15/30/40/game), making the sport intuitive for anyone with a racquet-sport background.

04

Social by nature

Because padel is nearly always played in pairs, it brings friends and members together. The social dynamic is a core part of the sport's identity.

How the game works

A simple breakdown for anyone unfamiliar with the sport.

The serve is an underhand bounce serve, played diagonally from behind the service line. This is one of the sport's most welcoming details — even beginners can put the ball in play comfortably within their first session.
Teams generally play side-by-side, moving forward to the net when possible and backward together when under pressure. The enclosed court creates a natural "playing corridor," making the game readable for spectators and learners.
A ball can bounce once on the floor, then rebound off the walls before being returned. Experienced players learn to read these ricochets and exploit them — which gives the sport a strategic depth not found in most racquet sports.
Most reservations run between 60 and 90 minutes, divided into clear arrival and departure windows. This block structure is central to the operating model — it keeps the building calm and predictable throughout the day.
Typical Club Operation

A structured, reservation-based rhythm

A well-run padel club functions less like a drop-in gym and more like a restaurant: guests book in advance, arrive for a defined window, and leave shortly after their session ends. Court access is managed through a scheduling app; walk-ins without a reservation are not typically accommodated. The atmosphere is active but calm — busy hours are concentrated, and off-peak hours are genuinely quiet.

Operational fingerprint

  • App-based reservations in fixed time blocks
  • Natural player turnover every 60–90 minutes
  • Staffing anchored by a single front-desk lead
  • Short lingering times; activity is the draw
  • Quiet off-peak windows between peak waves

Differences between pickleball & padel

Pickleball and padel are often grouped together, but they are meaningfully different sports — and they produce meaningfully different club operations. Studio Arenna is positioned firmly in the padel model: a sport-focused, reservation-based, community-driven format. The goal of this section is to explain that difference clearly and respectfully.

Court specifications — space planning differences

The physical footprint of padel is substantially larger than pickleball. In the same space, operators can typically fit approximately 2.3 pickleball courts for every one padel court. This has direct implications for density, cost, and operational style.

Subsection A

Operational differences: two club models

Each sport has produced its own club archetype. Both are valid. They just serve different uses and neighborhoods.

If you choose Padel

The Quality Club

  • Positioned as a sport-first, curated social environment.
  • High-turnover operation, with a more deliberate experience.
  • Requires a strict reservation system and an active community ecosystem.
  • Players expect locker rooms, a specialized pro shop, and a calm cafe-bar.
  • Relies on certified instructors — padel's learning curve is more technical than pickleball's.

If you choose Pickleball

The Volume & Community Club

  • High-turnover democratization model — broad, accessible appeal.
  • More open format and casual atmosphere.
  • Strong "open play" dynamic — players arrive alone and rotate partners.
  • Courts fill more easily without players needing to know each other.
  • Often operates as "eatertainment" — quick food, drinks, and music-forward vibe.
  • Less technical coaching demand for beginners; staff focuses on crowd flow.
Subsection B

Customer profile — who each sport attracts

Padel

A status and competition audience

  • Young professionals and executives
  • Former tennis players looking for something less solitary
  • Groups of friends looking for a weekend plan with a strong social vibe
  • Perceived as trendy and socially aspirational

Importantly, the target padel audience overlaps with tennis players and people coming from other fitness activities — rather than a casual drop-in crowd.

Pickleball

A mass, intergenerational audience

  • A broad, intergenerational sport
  • Grandparents, families, and players of all physical conditions
  • Barrier to entry is almost zero
  • Many people can genuinely play within 30 minutes of picking it up

Pickleball's strength is its accessibility — a meaningful social role that complements, rather than competes with, what padel offers.

Subsection C

The U.S. landscape in 2026

Pickleball

The participation titan

Pickleball has been integrated into mainstream American culture. It suits high-volume use, large tournaments, and steady throughput. Many clubs and gyms convert underutilized tennis courts into four pickleball courts to maximize revenue per square foot.

Padel

The rising premium player

Padel is growing rapidly as a sophisticated alternative. It does not yet carry pickleball's mass penetration, but it shows unusually high customer loyalty — the players who discover it tend to stay.

Framing this differently. Studio Arenna is more focused on sport and play than on heavy amenity-driven entertainment. The target audience overlaps with tennis players and fitness-oriented users. The model is quieter, more structured, and more reservation-driven than high-volume eatertainment venues. Both sports have a place — they simply produce different businesses.

The extreme theoretical ceiling

Padel is structurally limited to 4 players per court. With 5 courts, the absolute maximum simultaneous population on site is 20 players plus 1 front-desk staff member — 21 individuals. Even in the extreme hypothetical where every player arrives alone in a separate vehicle, total demand would be 21 parking spaces.

This assumes no carpools, no rideshare, no pedestrians, no cyclists, and all five courts full at once — conditions that rarely occur together.

Real-world data

Measured occupancy across U.S. padel clubs

Studio Arenna tracks hourly occupancy for more than 90% of U.S. padel clubs. Average club occupancy ranges between 30%–37% depending on the day — roughly 1.5 to 2 courts in use, or 6–8 players on site at any given time.

Only about 10–12 weekly hours reach above 70% occupancy, primarily in the 6:00–10:00 PM window. Even during those peaks, parking demand stays well below the 26 spaces provided.

Multimodal Access

Brushy Creek Trail on site

The property sits directly beside the Brushy Creek Trail and bike path, which meaningfully reduces vehicle dependence. Studio Arenna will install dedicated on-site parking for bicycles, scooters, and e-bikes, actively encouraging non-vehicle access. This pattern mirrors usage at urban padel clubs near trails in Denver, Miami, and Austin.

Shared-Ride Behavior

Groups, rideshare, and drop-offs

Padel players frequently travel in groups. Matches are often coordinated through mobile apps and social circles, producing a meaningful share of Ubers, shared rides, and drop-offs — further reducing parking demand during the busiest windows.

Complementary operating hours

The two tenants on the property operate on different daily rhythms. Square Cow Movers is a daytime, weekday-heavy operation; Studio Arenna is an evening-and-weekend operation. When Square Cow's lot is at its fullest, Studio Arenna is nearly empty — and vice versa.

Summary of findings

The study applied three distinct ITE methodologies — by number of employees, by number of courts, and by gross floor area — to generate a range of expected trip volumes. Even under a worst-case weekday PM peak-hour scenario using doubled court-based rates, the study estimates roughly 44 trips at the one-hour peak. The nature of reservation-based padel means half of those trips arrive in the final 10–15 minutes of an hour, and half depart in the first 10–15 minutes of the next — a pattern much less concentrated than a typical commuter rush.

Note: most analyses assumed 6 staff on-site — a worst-case overlap scenario. The more probable number of employees at any one time is 2 to 3.

Key findings

A.

Trip profile is non-concentrated

Half of peak-hour trips arrive in the last 10–15 minutes of an hour; half depart in the first 10–15 minutes of the next. Arrivals and departures do not pile up.

B.

Access geometry helps

A majority of traffic entering the site uses Brushy Creek Loop, a one-way service road — reducing the intensity of any incremental trips.

C.

Square Cow is shrinking

The existing tenant is reducing truck count, which will independently lower employee-driven trips from the property.

Consultant conclusion. The proposed Studio Arenna facility will produce a modest increase in trips relative to the current moving-and-storage use, with a worst-case combined peak of approximately 66 trips in the 7–9 AM weekday peak. Given the low number of added trips in the worst case, and the majority of traffic entering the site from Brushy Creek Loop (a one-way service road), the study finds no adverse effect on the surrounding street system.

Padel references in the area

To ground the proposal in something the City can verify, we provide a local reference point: Padel 39, one of the most established padel operations in the greater Austin area. Our intention here is not to point to events, parties, or tournaments — but to demonstrate that padel clubs can and do operate as structured community places where people come to play, compete, and socialize in an organized way.

Padel 39

Operational observations

Padel 39 operates as an active but orderly club: courts are booked in advance, players arrive for their reservation windows, and the building rhythm follows predictable peaks and lulls. The club hosts coaching, practice sessions, and social play in a structured, repeatable format.

What's relevant to the Cedar Park review is how these operations present at a neighborhood level — quiet outside of peak windows, organized during them, and with clear arrival and departure times driven by the reservation system rather than drop-in traffic.
